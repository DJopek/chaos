\section{Validation}
\label{sec:validation}
Before we try to bring about new results, we should verify our method. For validation, we decided to replicate some results of Sychrovsky~\cite{Sychrovsky2020} for the SCHW+BW system and compare some of our SCHW+BW basin maps with the Poincar\'e section from Polcar, Sukova and Semerak~\cite{Polcar2019}.

Our first validation case was Figure 4.3 in Sychrovsky~\cite{Sychrovsky2020}.

Our basin map visually corresponds to Figure 4.3 in Sychrovsky~\cite{Sychrovsky2020} with $\lambda_{\text{stop}} = 0.02$. This correspondence can be explained by the fact that in our implementation, the stopping criterion is based on the exceeded relative tolerance for the conserved quantities. Therefore, the particle can get as close to the singularity as allowed by the tolerance. The longer x86 integration in Figure~\ref{fig:basin-map-500-l3p75-e0p955-b20-M0p5-z0p2-x86} shows that part of the apparent basin structure is also affected by finite-time classification. When the same initial conditions are followed until \(\tau_{\mathrm{MAX}}=10^5M\), some trajectories that were previously classified as orbital are reclassified as colliding/plunged.

There are various reasons why our result may not fully correspond to the result of Sychrovsky~\cite{Sychrovsky2020}. In this case, the accessible region is divided into two parts. Hence, what we see in our zoomed basins are either eternal-orbit outcomes or trajectories terminating near the ring, but no plunges into the black hole. As mentioned by Sychrovsky \cite{Sychrovsky2020}, as $\lambda_{\text{stop}}$ increases, the basin of eternal orbits grows because the particles are allowed to move closer to the ring~\cite{Sychrovsky2020}. Other factors can also play a role, including different codebases and precision. As mentioned earlier, our solver uses \texttt{long double}. On Apple silicon ARM64 with Apple Clang 17, \texttt{long double} has the same numerical precision as \texttt{double}. On the x86\_64 Linux cluster with GCC 11.5, \texttt{long double} uses extended precision. We therefore continued our calculations on x86\_64 Linux cluster, which provides better parallelization and higher precision for this code.

    d_{\mathrm{ARM}} = 1.861 \pm 0.029,
    \qquad

    \label{eq:fractal-dimension-arm-x86}
\end{equation}
The high fractal dimension of the basin boundary in both cases, corresponding to the low uncertainty exponent, indicates a high degree of final-state sensitivity to perturbations of the initial conditions. However, these values are not consistent with the values from Sychrovsky~\cite{Sychrovsky2020}. The discussion above for visual inconsistency applies here as well.

\subsection{Mass dependence}
\label{sec:schw-bw-sychrovsky-mass-dependence}

Figure 5.18 in Sychrovsky \cite{Sychrovsky2020} shows the dependence of SCHW+BW basin maps on
the Bach-Weyl ring mass for \(b=20M\), \(l=3.75M\), and \(z=0.2M\).
Although the caption in Sychrovsky's thesis states \(\epsilon=0.955\),
the shape of the accessible region is consistent with
\(\epsilon=0.977\). We therefore treat \(0.977\) as the energy used for
that figure~\cite{Sychrovsky2020}. As $\mathcal{M} \rightarrow0$ we expect $d \rightarrow1$ as the system goes to the Schwarzschild limit, which is integrable. However, even a light ring can significantly affect the dynamics, which can be described as "cracks" in the basin $O$. This effect can be observed in our basin maps. We can also observe the formation of smaller islands and their merging, which results in a decrease in the dimenion.

The visual comparison is only partially successful. However, the trend is consistent as we can observe a peak of the dimension with the appearance of "cracks", a decrease of the dimension with heavier rings, and the
unperturbed case \(\mathcal{M}=0\) gives a dimension close to that of a
smooth curve, as expected for the Schwarzschild limit. However, 
non-zero low-mass cases do not show a clean monotone approach
\(d\to1\) as \(\mathcal{M}\to0^+\). Instead, several small but non-zero
ring masses give dimensions in the range \(d\approx 1.6\)-\(1.7\). Nevertheless, we can see that the error increases as the ring becomes lighter. It is possible that to better resolve the dimension of the structure we would need a much finer resolution and the decline would be observed at even smaller values for $\mathcal{M}$.

The result is still
useful for the thesis because it shows that the method detects a regular
boundary in the exactly integrable Schwarzschild case and follows the trend, but it also shows limitations where more careful analysis is needed.

\subsection{Comparison with Poincar\'e sections}

As a second validation, we compare our basin maps with the equatorial
Poincar\'e sections shown by Polcar, Sukova, and Semerak~\cite{Polcar2019}.
Their sections were computed for SCHW+BW geodesics with
\(\ell=3.75M\), \(\epsilon=0.977\), and ring radius \(b=20M\), while
the ring mass \(\mathcal{M}\) was varied.

\begin{figure}[H]

To check that the same trajectory integrator reproduces the expected
phase-space structures, we also computed two corresponding Poincar\'e sections
with \textit{Gravitacek 2}; see
Figures~\ref{fig:poincare-schw-bw-gravitacek2-M0p1}

\section{Reissner-Nordstr\"om--Majumdar-Papapetrou}
\label{sec:rn-mp-results}

We now consider the extreme Reissner-Nordstr\"om black hole surrounded
by a Majumdar-Papapetrou ring. The analysis by Polcar and Semerak \cite{PolcarSemerak2019} using the Melnikov method shows that for weak
perturbations, the transversal intersection of stable and unstable manifolds along the homoclinic orbit is present. As a result of this, the chaotic dynamics appears. Our purpose here is not to reproduce any results but rather to bring about a new view into the dynamics. 

As discussed in \ref{subchap:RNMPanalysis} a specific condition needs to be taken into account in order to have basin maps where a particle may plunge into the black hole and energy levels with given parameters may affect the results. Before we dive into a closer analysis of how this manifests in basin maps, we will take a look at some nuances. 

As before, precision and integration time may affect the overall result. We will now work with fixed $z$, so only a collision with the ring is possible, no plunging into the black hole. First, a reference run is done on the ARM architecture, therefore, with lower precision with a standard integration time $\tau_{\mathrm{MAX}} = 10^4M$. Then runs with integration times $\tau_{\mathrm{MAX}}=10^5M$ both on ARM and x86 are done.

We will now analyze the basin maps with the possibility of a test particle plunging into the black hole. We will choose such parameters that the accessible region will not be separated into subregions. In addition, we will start with energies close to the energy level tangental to the local maximum of $V_{ef}$, so near the separatrix, and we will decrease it, analyzing the dependence of the fractal dimension $d$ on the energy $\epsilon$. As \cite{PolcarSemerak2019} suggests, we may expect a chaotic nature near the separatrix, and as the energy increases, we may expect $d \rightarrow1$.

The RN+MP sequence shows a clear energy dependence. With energ levels near the separatrix, the basin map is rich and, as noticed earlier with a comparison of the basin maps and Poincaré sections, the island in the basin $C$ may suggest a chaotic nature. However, with the energy level going even higher we can see dominance of the basin $O$ on the map and shrinking of the basin $C$, both with a smoother structure. This qualitative analysis suggests that our earlier hypothesis may be possible, and we shall proceed with a qualitative analysis.

The dependence of the fractal dimension $d$ on the energy $\epsilon$ quantitatively supports our qualitative observation. We see a monotonic decline in the fractal dimension $d$ as the energy level increases, further away from the separatrix. Near the separatrix, we see a high outcome sensitivity, possibly even initial condition sensitivity as the structure of the basin map suggests. As we move further away from the separatrix, we see that the system is becoming integrable. 

Given the analysis done in \cite{PolcarSemerak2019} and the above results, we may form a hypothesis. Near the separatrix, for large perturbation, given the parameters with the condition of plunge into the black hole being possible, a chaos of homoclinic origin is present. However, the above analysis suggests this, it does not confirm it. Further research and analysis, for example, with Poincaré sections, is needed.
