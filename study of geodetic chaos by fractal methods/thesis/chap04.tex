\section{Validation}
\label{sec:validation}
Before we try to bring about new results, we should verify our method. For validation, we decided to replicate some results of Sychrovsky~\cite{Sychrovsky2020} for the Schwarzschild--Bach--Weyl (SCHW+BW) system and compare some of our SCHW+BW basin maps with the Poincar\'e section from Polcar, Sukova and Semerak~\cite{Polcar2019}.

Our basin map visually corresponds to Figure 4.3 in Sychrovsky~\cite{Sychrovsky2020} with $\lambda_{\text{stop}} = 0.02$. This correspondence can be explained by the fact that in our implementation, the stopping criterion is based on the exceeded relative tolerance for the conserved quantities. Therefore, the particle can get as close to the singularity as allowed by the tolerance.

There are various reasons why our result may not fully correspond to the result of Sychrovsky~\cite{Sychrovsky2020}. In this case, the accessible region is divided into two parts. Hence, what we see in our zoomed basins are either eternal-orbit outcomes or trajectories terminating near the ring, but no falls into the black hole. As mentioned by Sychrovsky, as $\lambda_{\text{stop}}$ increases, the basin of eternal orbits grows because the particles are allowed to move closer to the ring~\cite{Sychrovsky2020}. Our stopping criterion is not identical to Sychrovsky's \(\lambda_{\text{stop}}\): it combines proximity events with conservation-error events. The visual similarity to results with larger \(\lambda_{\text{stop}}\) is therefore an interpretation, not a direct parameter equivalence. Other factors can also play a role, including the different codebases and floating-point precision. As mentioned earlier, our solver uses \texttt{long double}. On Apple silicon ARM64 with Apple Clang 17, \texttt{long double} has the same numerical precision as \texttt{double}. On the x86\_64 Linux cluster with GCC 11.5, \texttt{long double} uses extended precision. We therefore continued our calculations on x86\_64, which provides better parallelization and higher arithmetic precision for this code.

The high fractal dimension of the basin boundary in both cases, corresponding to the low uncertainty exponent, indicates a high degree of final-state sensitivity to perturbations of the initial conditions. However, these values are not consistent with the values from Sychrovsky~\cite{Sychrovsky2020}. The discussion above for the visual inconsistency applies here as well.

\subsection{Mass dependence compared with Sychrovsky Figure 5.18}
\label{sec:schw-bw-sychrovsky-mass-dependence}

Sychrovsky's Figure 5.18 shows the dependence of SCHW+BW basin maps on
the Bach--Weyl ring mass for \(b=20M\), \(l=3.75M\), \(z=0.2M\), and
\(\epsilon=0.955\)~\cite{Sychrovsky2020}. The associated discussion and
Figure 5.10 in that thesis use these maps to argue that the basin-boundary
Minkowski dimension tends to one as the ring mass tends to zero. This is
the behavior expected from the integrable Schwarzschild limit: when the
external ring is removed, the additional non-integrable perturbation
vanishes and the outcome boundary should become smooth.

The result set shown here was computed with the same black-hole mass,
angular momentum, ring radius and initial plane, but with
\(\epsilon=0.977\). It should therefore be understood as a test of the same
mass-dependence mechanism, not as an exact reproduction of Sychrovsky's
Figure 5.18. Only x86 calculations are used. The two low-mass maps with
\(\mathcal{M}=0.003M\) and \(\mathcal{M}=0.006M\) are omitted from the
published comparison.

The visual comparison is only partially successful. The maps show that the
structure of the basin boundary changes strongly with \(\mathcal{M}\), and the
unperturbed case \(\mathcal{M}=0\) gives a dimension close to that of a
smooth curve, as expected for the Schwarzschild limit. However, the
non-zero low-mass cases do not show a clean monotone approach
\(d\to1\) as \(\mathcal{M}\to0^+\). Instead, several small but non-zero
ring masses give dimensions in the range \(d\simeq1.6\)--\(1.7\).

This means that the result validates the integrable limit only in the strict
\(\mathcal{M}=0\) calculation, while the limiting trend inferred by
Sychrovsky is not reproduced quantitatively in this run. The most likely
reasons are methodological rather than physical: the energy is not identical
to Sychrovsky's Figure 5.18, our stopping criterion is not the same as
Sychrovsky's \(\lambda_{\text{stop}}\), and the uncertainty-exponent
calculation samples a finite grid and a coordinate-direction perturbation.
For very weak perturbations, a thin chaotic layer or a numerical
classification layer can therefore dominate the measured uncertain fraction
before the asymptotic smooth-boundary limit is resolved. The result is still
useful for the thesis because it shows that the method detects a regular
boundary in the exactly integrable Schwarzschild case, but it also warns that
small-perturbation limits require more careful convergence tests than a single
\(500\times500\) basin-map sequence can provide.

\subsection{Comparison with Poincar\'e sections}

As a second validation of the Schwarzschild black-hole plus Bach--Weyl
ring implementation, we compare our basin maps with the equatorial
Poincar\'e sections shown by Polcar, Sukova and Semerak~\cite{Polcar2019}.
Their sections were computed for geodesics with
\(\ell=3.75M\), \(\epsilon=0.977\), and ring radius \(b=20M\), while
the relative ring mass \(\mathcal{M}/M\) was varied. The reference
Poincar\'e sections are reproduced in Figure~\ref{fig:poincare-schw-bw-chaos-v}.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{img/poincare_sections_schw_bw_chaos_V.png}

    Schwarzschild black hole with a Bach--Weyl ring, with
    \(b=20M\), \(\ell=3.75M\), and \(\epsilon=0.977\). The values of
    \(\mathcal{M}/M\) are indicated in the individual panels. Figure
    taken from Polcar, Sukova and Semerak~\cite{Polcar2019}.}
    \label{fig:poincare-schw-bw-chaos-v}
\end{figure}

For the comparison we use only the x86 calculations, since these were
computed with extended precision \texttt{long double}. The basin maps in
Figure~\ref{fig:basin-schw-bw-chaos-v-x86} use the same values of
\(\ell\), \(\epsilon\), \(b\), and the equatorial plane \(z=0\). The
ring-mass values are those available in our x86 result set, so this is a
comparison of the trend with increasing perturbation, not a
panel-by-panel reproduction of the exact masses in
Figure~\ref{fig:poincare-schw-bw-chaos-v}. They are also not Poincar\'e
sections: each pixel represents an initial condition in the
\((\rho_0,u^\rho_0)\) plane and is classified according to the final
outcome of the numerical integration. In addition, the reference sections
use Schwarzschild \(r\), while our basin maps are plotted in Weyl
\(\rho\). Nevertheless, the comparison is useful because both diagnostics
probe the equatorial reduced phase space and both react visibly to the
increasing Bach--Weyl perturbation.

The comparison is necessarily qualitative. A Poincar\'e section separates
regular invariant curves, resonant islands and stochastic layers inside
the bounded part of phase space, whereas the basin map records whether a
chosen initial condition survives until \(T_{\mathrm{MAX}}\) or reaches
the singular part of the space-time earlier. Therefore one should not
expect a one-to-one correspondence between individual islands in
Figure~\ref{fig:poincare-schw-bw-chaos-v} and coloured domains in
Figure~\ref{fig:basin-schw-bw-chaos-v-x86}. The relevant point is the
common trend. In the reference sections, increasing \(\mathcal{M}\)
destroys the simple near-integrable structure: invariant curves are
gradually replaced by resonant island chains and broader chaotic layers.
In our x86 basin maps the same increase of the perturbing ring mass
produces a progressively more complicated basin boundary, especially
near the edge of the accessible region and near the ring. This supports
the interpretation that the final-state sensitivity visible in the basin
maps is associated with the same loss of regularity that is seen directly
in the Poincar\'e sections.

\section{Schwarzschild--Bach--Weyl Radius Dependence}
\label{sec:schw-bw-radius-dependence}

radius. Polcar, Sukova and Semerak also show the complementary dependence
on the ring radius \(b\) for fixed \(\mathcal{M}=0.5M\),
\(\ell=3.75M\), and \(\epsilon=0.94\)~\cite{Polcar2019}. Their
Poincar\'e sections are reproduced in
Figure~\ref{fig:poincare-schw-bw-chaos-v-radius}. The sections show that
changing \(b\) does not merely rescale the accessible region. It changes
the resonance structure in the bounded phase space, with different
island chains and chaotic layers appearing for different ring radii.

\section{Reissner--Nordstr\"om--Majumdar--Papapetrou Results}
\label{sec:rn-mp-results}

We now consider the extreme Reissner--Nordstr\"om black hole surrounded
by a Majumdar--Papapetrou ring. We abbreviate this configuration as the
RN+MP system. It was studied by Polcar,
Sukova and Semerak as an electrovacuum analogue of the
Schwarzschild--Bach--Weyl system~\cite{Polcar2019}. It was also
analysed by Polcar and Semerak using the Melnikov method, where the
splitting of the stable and unstable manifolds of a perturbed homoclinic
orbit gives an analytic mechanism for the onset of chaos in the
RN+MP system~\cite{PolcarSemerak2019}. Our purpose here is not to reproduce
their Poincar\'e sections, but to use basin maps and fractal dimensions
as a complementary final-state diagnostic.

The basin maps in
Figures~\ref{fig:basin-rn-mp-energy-low-x86}--\ref{fig:basin-rn-mp-energy-high-x86}
were computed for \(M=1\), \(\ell=3.2M\), \(b=20M\), and
\(\mathcal{M}=0.5M\), while the energy \(\epsilon\) was varied. The
ring radius \(b\) is the Reissner--Nordstr\"om radial coordinate; the
corresponding Majumdar--Papapetrou cylindrical ring radius is
\(\sigma=b-M\). The maps use \(z(\rho)=n\sqrt{M\rho}\), as described in
Chapter~\ref{chap:implementation}. For each energy, the left panel shows the
wider basin map and the right panel shows the close-up used for the
fractal-dimension calculation. The wider and close-up panels do not always use
the same value of \(n\), because they sample different parts of the accessible
region. The maps excluded from publication during the exploratory stage, namely the
\(b=15M\), \(\ell=3.75M\), \(\epsilon=0.94\), \(z=0\) trial runs, are
not used in these figures or in the following fractal-dimension
analysis.

The RN+MP sequence shows a clear energy dependence. For
\(\epsilon=0.955\)--\(0.965\), the accessible part of the sampled
initial-condition plane contains strongly interwoven basins both in the
wider view and in the close-up, indicating large final-state sensitivity.
These lower energies are close to the regime selected in
Figure~\ref{fig:rn-mp-veff-z-of-rho}, where the energy level lies just above
the local maximum of the effective potential. In the unperturbed problem this
maximum corresponds to an unstable circular orbit and its separatrix. After the
Majumdar--Papapetrou ring perturbation, the Melnikov analysis predicts that the
stable and unstable manifolds associated with this separatrix split and form a
homoclinic tangle~\cite{PolcarSemerak2019}. The strongly interwoven basins are
therefore naturally interpreted as final-state traces of this homoclinic chaotic
layer. At higher energies the boundary remains nontrivial, but the close-up
panels show reduced interweaving and the visually dominant regions become more
coherent. This is consistent with the fact that homoclinic chaos is localized
near the separatrix: moving the sampled energy surface away from the most
relevant separatrix neighbourhood can reduce the observed final-state
sensitivity~\cite{PolcarThesis2018,Witzany2015,PolcarSemerak2019}.

basin boundary for the same energy sequence. The resulting values are
listed in Table~\ref{tab:rn-mp-fractal-dimensions} and plotted in
Figure~\ref{fig:rn-mp-fractal-dimension-energy}.

The fractal dimension stays close to \(d\simeq1.81\) for
\(\epsilon=0.955\)--\(0.965\), which corresponds to a highly fractal
basin boundary. It then decreases as the energy is increased, reaching
\(d=1.079\pm0.036\) at \(\epsilon=0.995\). The small increase from
\(\epsilon=0.970\) to \(0.975\) should not be interpreted as a separate
monotonic law; it is more naturally read as local structure in the
sampled energy interval. The main result is the overall loss of
fractal complexity across the sequence. In this sense, the basin maps
add a quantitative final-state perspective to the RN+MP dynamics
studied in Refs.~\cite{Polcar2019,PolcarSemerak2019}: the presence of a
chaotic mechanism does not fix the observed basin-boundary dimension,
which depends on the selected energy surface and the sampled
initial-condition region. In particular, our results are compatible with the
picture emphasized in the Melnikov analysis and in the thesis treatments of
homoclinic chaos: the perturbation creates a chaotic layer near the broken
separatrix, but the size of the layer sampled by a basin map depends on the
chosen constants of motion and on the section of phase space used for the
initial conditions~\cite{PolcarThesis2018,Witzany2015,PolcarSemerak2019}.
