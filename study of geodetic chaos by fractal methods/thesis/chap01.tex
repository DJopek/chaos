\chapter{Chaos}
\label{chap:chaos}

In this chapter, we will introduce the necessary mathematical theory and concepts from chaos theory that we will work with and that are relevant for this thesis.

\section{Outcome basin}
In dynamical systems, the concept of attractor can be described as a subset of a phase space toward which a system tends to evolve. An attractor's basin of attraction is the subset of the phase space such that any point (any initial condition) in that region will asymptotically evolve into the attractor. In the dynamical system of a black hole with a ring,
the test particle can evolve towards collision with the black hole or with the ring, escape to infinity or eternal orbit. An evolution towards eternal orbit or escape to infinity does not really satisfy the concept of an attractor. In order to capture and examine this behavior, we need to define what we will refer to as an outcome basin. 

\begin{defn}[Outcome basin]
An outcome basin is the set of initial conditions, being a subset of the phase space, that evolve towards a given outcome.
\end{defn}

In our case, by outcome we understand eternal orbit, collision with a ring or plunge into a black hole, and escape to infinity.

\section{Basin boundary}
Let us now consider a specific system that has only two outcomes, hence two outcome basins. Denote the basin of the collision/plunge outcome by $C$ and $O$ as the basin of the eternal orbit outcome. By the boundary of these basins we understand the following.

\begin{defn}[Basin boundary]
Let \(O\) and \(C\) be two outcome basins. The subset \(\Sigma\) of the phase space that separates these two basins is called a basin boundary.
\end{defn}

For the systems considered in this thesis, a smooth basin boundary is associated
with predictable outcome assignment, whereas a fractal basin boundary indicates
outcome sensitivity, or rather degradation, in one's ability to determine which outcome is approached. This is discussed more in \cite{Grebogi1983} and in \cite{Ott2002}.

\section{Homoclinic tangles}
\label{sec:homoclinic-tangles}

By hyperbolic (fixed) point $\bar{x}$ we understand such a fixed point that when we do a linearization around this point, the corresponding Jacobian matrix has no eigenvalue with zero real part. With hyperbolic fixed points, the concepts of stable and unstable manifolds and homoclinic points are associated. A hypersurface in phase space, which tends to equilibrium as $t \rightarrow \infty$ is called a stable manifold. The unstable manifold is defined as a hypersurface in the phase space, which tends to equilibrium as $t \rightarrow -\infty$.
The manifolds can intersect transversally under a perturbation and form the so-called homoclinic points. A full orbit of a homoclinic point is called a homoclinic orbit. Along this orbit, when the system is perturbed, a transverse intersection of the manifolds is present. This is a signature of chaotic dynamics, as described by the Smale-Birkhoff homoclinic theorem. More on this topic can be found in \cite{Wiggins2003}.
Chaos of homoclinic origin in geodesic dynamics in systems with perturbed black holes was discussed and studied in \cite{PolcarSemerak2019}, \cite{PolcarThesis2018} and \cite{Witzany2015}. In particular, The Melnikov method was applied in \cite{PolcarSemerak2019} and resulted in the finding of simple zeros, confirming the transversal intersection along the homoclinic orbit, which implies the occurrence of chaotic behavior.

\section{Fractals}
A fractal is a set whose structure remains nontrivial even when examined at
increasingly smaller scales. A fractal exhibits similar patterns at increasingly smaller scales. This is known as self-similarity. In order to quantify this self-similarity and roughness on smaller scales, we define the fractal dimensions. Several inequivalent definitions exist. The standard
mathematical definition is the Hausdorff dimension. For a set \(X\), it is given
by
\[
\dim_{\mathrm H}(X)
\coloneqq
\inf\left\{
d \geq 0 \,\middle|\, \mathcal{H}^{d}(X)=0
\right\},
\]
where \(\mathcal{H}^{d}\) is the Hausdorff measure of dimension \(d\). This
definition is useful conceptually because it generalizes the concept of
dimension as fractals have a non-intiger (fractal) dimension usually exceeding the topological dimension. However, in numerical applications, it is often
more practical to use the box-counting dimension, as we will see later.

The box-counting dimension, also known as the Minkowski dimension, is defined as
follows.

\begin{defn}[Box-counting dimension]
Let \(S \subset \mathbb{R}^n\) be a bounded set and let
\(N(\varepsilon)\) denote the minimum number of n-dimensional boxes of side length
\(\varepsilon\) required to cover \(S\). The box-counting
dimension, also known as the Minkowski dimension, is defined by
\[
d \equiv \dim_{\mathrm{B}}(S)
\coloneqq
\lim_{\varepsilon \to 0}
\frac{\ln N(\varepsilon)}
     {\ln(1/\varepsilon)},
\]
provided that the limit exists.
\end{defn}

Roughly speaking, this means that the number \(N(\varepsilon)\) of
\(n\)-dimensional boxes of side length \(\varepsilon\) needed to cover the set
scales like
\[
    N(\varepsilon)\sim C\varepsilon^{-d}
\]
as \(\varepsilon\) becomes small.

With an n-dimensional phase space and a basin boundary that separates two basins, $d\rightarrow n-1$ is related to the assignment of predictable outcomes, while a boundary of the fractal basin with $d \rightarrow n$ is related to the outcome sensitivity.

\section{Uncertainty exponent}
\label{sec:uncertainty-exponent}
Suppose that we perturb a random initial condition by a small amount, 
$\varepsilon>0$, in a random direction. If the new trajectory ends up in a different basin from the unperturbed one, then the initial condition is called $\varepsilon$-uncertain.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth]{img/region.png}
    \caption{Schematic region of phase space divided by a basin boundary into two basins. Points 1 and 2 illustrate initial conditions with uncertainty \(\varepsilon\) (Grebogi et al.~\cite{Grebogi1983}).}
    \label{fig:uncertain-initial-conditions}
\end{figure}

If we denote $\Omega$ a subset of a phase space in which we are interested and $U_{\varepsilon} \subset \Omega$ the set of uncertain initial conditions, we can define a ratio $f(\varepsilon) := \frac{Vol(U_{\varepsilon})}{Vol(\Omega)}$.
Then we define a value $\alpha \coloneqq n-d$, where $n$ is the dimension of the phase space and $d$ is the box-counting dimension of the basin boundary.

Following the uncertainty-exponent argument described by Grebogi et al.~\cite{Grebogi1983} and Ott~\cite[p.~206]{Ott2002},

\begin{equation}
  f \sim \varepsilon^{\alpha}.
  \label{eq:uncertainty-scaling}
\end{equation}

Therefore, we will refer to $\alpha$ as the uncertainty exponent. We can also rewrite \eqref{eq:uncertainty-scaling} as follows

\begin{equation}
    \ln{f} = \ln{k} + \alpha\ln{\varepsilon}.
    \label{eq:uncertainty-log-fit}
\end{equation}

This form will be very useful in practice. Once we construct a basin map, we
can select a smaller region of it. Consider an initial condition
\((x_0,v_0)\). We perturb the coordinate direction of the basin map, so the perturbed initial
conditions are \((x_0+\varepsilon,v_0)\) and
\((x_0-\varepsilon,v_0)\). We check to which basin these new initial conditions correspond. If either of
them does not correspond to the same basin as the original initial condition, we
classify the original initial condition as uncertain. In this way, we perturb
every initial condition in the selected region (every sampled initial condition) and calculate how many of them are
uncertain, denoting this number by \(N_U\). We define a ratio of the number of
uncertain initial conditions to the total number of sampled initial conditions
\(N\) as \(\bar{f}(\varepsilon):=\frac{N_U}{N}\). This fraction can be interpreted as
the probability that a point chosen from the sampled region is uncertain with
respect to the chosen perturbation. As the number of sampled points
increases and the discretization becomes finer, \(\bar{f}(\varepsilon)\)
approaches the value \(f(\varepsilon)\). When
we repeat this process for more values of \(\varepsilon\), we can perform a
linear fit based on \eqref{eq:uncertainty-log-fit} to obtain the uncertainty
exponent \(\alpha\). Then, from the definition of $\alpha$ we calculate the fractal dimension $d$.
