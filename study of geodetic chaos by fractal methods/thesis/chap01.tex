\chapter{Chaos}
\label{chap:chaos}

In this chapter, we will introduce the necessary mathematical theory and concepts from chaos theory that we will work with and that are relevant for this thesis.

\section{Dynamical systems}
\label{sec:dynamical-systems}

Following the terminology used by Wiggins~\cite[pp.~1--4]{Wiggins2003},
a dynamical system is a mathematical rule that determines how a state
evolves with respect to an independent variable, usually interpreted as
time. In continuous time this rule is written as an ordinary differential
equation
\begin{equation}
    \dot{x}=f(x,t;\mu),
    \label{eq:continuous-dynamical-system}
\end{equation}
where \(x\in U\subset\mathbb{R}^{n}\) is the state, \(t\in\mathbb{R}\) is
time, and \(\mu\) denotes parameters. If \(f\) does not depend explicitly on
time, the system is called autonomous. In discrete time the evolution is
given by a map
\begin{equation}
    x_{n+1}=g(x_n;\mu).
    \label{eq:discrete-dynamical-system}
\end{equation}
Both vector fields of the form \eqref{eq:continuous-dynamical-system} and
maps of the form \eqref{eq:discrete-dynamical-system} are dynamical
systems.

\begin{defn}[Dynamical system]
A dynamical system is a continuous-time vector field or a discrete-time map
that specifies the evolution of states in a phase space.
\end{defn}

The phase space is the space of dependent variables of the system. A
solution of \eqref{eq:continuous-dynamical-system} is a curve
\(x(t)\) in phase space satisfying the differential equation. Such a curve
is also called a trajectory. The orbit through an initial condition \(x_0\)
is the set of phase-space points visited by the corresponding trajectory,
\[
    O(x_0)=\{x(t,t_0,x_0)\mid t \in I\},
\]
where \(I\) is the interval on which the solution exists. For a map, the
orbit of \(x_0\) is the sequence
\[
    x_0,\quad g(x_0),\quad g^2(x_0),\quad \ldots .
\]
In this thesis the geodesic equations define a continuous-time dynamical
system, while Poincar\'e sections and basin maps give reduced
representations of its behavior in selected parts of phase space.

\section{Outcome basin}
In dynamical systems, the concept of attractor can be described as a subset of a phase space toward which a system tends to evolve. An attractor's basin of attraction is the subset of the phase space such that any point (any initial condition) in that region will asymptotically evolve into the attractor. In the dynamical system of a black hole with a ring,
the test particle can evolve towards collision with the black hole or with the ring, escape to infinity or eternal orbit. An evolution towards eternal orbit or escape to infinity does not really satisfy the concept of an attractor. In order to capture and examine this behavior, we need to define what we will refer to as an outcome basin. 

\begin{defn}[Outcome basin]
An outcome basin is the set of initial conditions, i.e. a subset of the phase space, that evolve towards a given outcome.
\end{defn}

In our case, by outcome we understand eternal orbit, collision with a black hole or a ring, and escape to infinity.

\section{Basin boundary}
Let us now consider a specific system that has only two outcomes, hence two outcome basins. Denote the basin of the collision outcome by $C$ and $O$ as the basin of the eternal orbit outcome. By the boundary of these basins we understand the following:

\begin{defn}[Basin boundary]
Let \(O\) and \(C\) be two outcome basins. The subset \(\Sigma\) of the phase space that separates these two basins is called a basin boundary.
\end{defn}

For the systems considered in this thesis, a smooth basin boundary is associated
with predictable final-state assignment, while a fractal basin boundary indicates
final-state sensitivity. This should not be read as a general equivalence between
``chaos'' and ``fractal boundary'' in all dynamical systems; rather, fractal basin
boundaries are one of the standard signatures of chaotic scattering and
unpredictability of outcomes~\cite{Grebogi1983,Ott2002}.

\section{Homoclinic tangles}
\label{sec:homoclinic-tangles}

One common mechanism by which chaotic scattering appears is the splitting of a
separatrix attached to an unstable periodic orbit. In a Poincar\'e map, such an
orbit appears as a hyperbolic fixed point \(p\). Its stable manifold \(W^s(p)\)
consists of points approaching \(p\) under forward iteration, while its unstable
manifold \(W^u(p)\) consists of points approaching \(p\) under backward
iteration. A homoclinic point is a point, different from \(p\), that lies in
\(W^s(p)\cap W^u(p)\). Equivalently, the corresponding orbit leaves the
neighbourhood of the hyperbolic orbit and returns to it asymptotically in both
time directions~\cite{Wiggins2003,PolcarThesis2018}.

If the stable and unstable manifolds coincide, the homoclinic orbit forms a
separatrix between qualitatively different motions. If a perturbation makes
these manifolds intersect transversely, the intersections are repeated under the
map and form a homoclinic tangle. By the Smale--Birkhoff homoclinic theorem,
the dynamics in a neighbourhood of such transverse homoclinic points contains a
horseshoe-type invariant set and is chaotic~\cite{Wiggins2003,PolcarThesis2018}.
For nearly integrable systems, however, this statement is local: the chaotic
layer may occupy only a small region close to the original separatrix, while
regular islands or invariant curves may survive elsewhere in phase space
\cite{PolcarThesis2018,Witzany2015}.

This mechanism is directly relevant to black-hole geodesic dynamics. In the
unperturbed black-hole problem, unstable circular geodesics play the role of
hyperbolic orbits, and the associated homoclinic orbits separate plunging motion
from bound motion. The Melnikov method tests whether a weak external source
splits the stable and unstable manifolds of this separatrix. In the black-hole
plus ring systems studied by Polcar and Semer\'ak, simple zeros of the Melnikov
function indicate transverse intersections and therefore a chaotic layer of
homoclinic origin~\cite{PolcarSemerak2019}.

\section{Fractals}
A fractal is a set whose structure remains nontrivial when it is examined at
smaller and smaller scales. In dynamical systems, fractal geometry is useful
because basin boundaries generated by chaotic scattering may be highly folded:
zooming in near the boundary reveals new alternations between outcomes rather
than a smooth separating curve. Fractal dimension is a way of quantifying this
scale dependence. Several inequivalent definitions exist. The standard
mathematical definition is the Hausdorff dimension. For a set \(X\), it is given
by
\[
\dim_{\mathrm H}(X)
\coloneqq
\inf\left\{
d \geq 0 \,\middle|\, \mathcal{H}^{d}(X)=0
\right\},
\]
where \(\mathcal{H}^{d}\) is the \(d\)-dimensional Hausdorff measure. This
definition is useful conceptually because it generalizes the usual integer
dimension to irregular sets. In numerical applications, however, it is often
more practical to use box-counting dimension, which is directly connected to the
uncertainty-exponent method used for the basin maps in this thesis
\cite{Ott2002,Wiggins2003}.

The box-counting dimension, also known as the Minkowski dimension, is defined as
follows.

\begin{defn}[Box-counting dimension]
Let \(S \subset \mathbb{R}^n\) be a bounded set and let
\(N(\varepsilon)\) denote the minimum number of n-dimensional boxes of side length
\(\varepsilon\) required to cover \(S\). The Minkowski
dimension, also called the box-counting dimension, is defined by
\[
d \equiv \dim_{\mathrm{B}}(S)
\coloneqq
\lim_{\varepsilon \to 0}
\frac{\ln N(\varepsilon)}
     {\ln(1/\varepsilon)},
\]
provided that the limit exists.
\end{defn}

Roughly speaking, this means that the number \(N(\varepsilon)\) of
\(n\)-dimensional boxes of side length \(\varepsilon\) needed to cover the set
scales like
\[
    N(\varepsilon)\sim C\varepsilon^{-d}
\]
as \(\varepsilon\) becomes small.

In our application, the important point is not the classification of fractals in
full mathematical generality, but whether the basin boundary has dimension
larger than that of a smooth curve in the two-dimensional basin map. A value
\(d>1\) indicates a boundary with non-smooth structure and final-state
sensitivity at small scales.

\section{Uncertainty exponent}
\label{sec:uncertainty-exponent}
Suppose that we perturb a random initial condition by a small amount, 
$\varepsilon$, in a random direction. If the new trajectory ends up in a different basin from the unperturbed one, then the initial condition is called $\varepsilon$-uncertain.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.72\linewidth]{img/region.png}
    \caption{Schematic region of phase space divided by a basin boundary into two basins of attraction. Initial conditions 1 and 2 illustrate the effect of uncertainty \(\varepsilon\) near the basin boundary. Adapted from Grebogi et al.~\cite{Grebogi1983}.}
    \label{fig:uncertain-initial-conditions}
\end{figure}

If we denote $\Omega$ a subset of a phase space in which we are interested and $U_{\varepsilon} \subset \Omega$ the set of uncertain initial conditions, we can define a ratio $f(\varepsilon) := \frac{Vol(U_{\varepsilon})}{Vol(\Omega)}$.
Then let us define a value $\alpha \coloneqq n-d$, where $n$ is the dimension of the phase space and $d$ is the box counting dimension of the basin boundary.
Following the uncertainty-exponent argument described by Grebogi et al.~\cite{Grebogi1983} and Ott~\cite[p.~206]{Ott2002},

\begin{equation}
  \bar{f} \sim \varepsilon^{\alpha}.
  \label{eq:uncertainty-scaling}
\end{equation}

Thus, we will refer to the value $\alpha$ as the uncertainty exponent. We can also rewrite \eqref{eq:uncertainty-scaling} as

\begin{equation}
    \ln{\bar{f}} = \ln{k} + \alpha\ln{\varepsilon}.
    \label{eq:uncertainty-log-fit}
\end{equation}

This form will be very useful in practice. Once we construct a basin map, we
can select a smaller region of it. Consider an initial condition
\((x_0,v_0)\). In the numerical implementation used in this thesis we perturb
only the coordinate direction of the basin map, so the perturbed initial
conditions are \((x_0+\varepsilon,v_0)\) and
\((x_0-\varepsilon,v_0)\). This is a coordinate-direction version of the
uncertainty-exponent estimate, not an average over all perturbation directions.
We check to which basin these new initial conditions correspond. If either of
them does not correspond to the same basin as the original initial condition, we
classify the original initial condition as uncertain. In this way, we perturb
every initial condition in the selected region and calculate how many of them are
uncertain, denoting this number by \(N_U\). We define a ratio of the number of
uncertain initial conditions to the total number of sampled initial conditions
\(N\) as \(\bar{f}(\varepsilon):=N_U/N\). This fraction can be interpreted as
the probability that a point chosen from the sampled region is uncertain with
respect to the chosen perturbation direction. As the number of sampled points
increases and the discretization becomes finer, \(\bar{f}(\varepsilon)\)
approaches the corresponding directional estimate of \(f(\varepsilon)\). When
we repeat this process for more values of \(\varepsilon\), we can perform a
linear fit based on \eqref{eq:uncertainty-log-fit} to obtain the uncertainty
exponent \(\alpha\). Since our basin maps are two-dimensional, the corresponding
box-counting dimension estimate is \(d=2-\alpha\).
