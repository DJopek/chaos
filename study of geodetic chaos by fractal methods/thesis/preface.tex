\chapter*{Introduction}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Introduction}

Black holes are usually introduced through highly symmetric exact solutions of
Einstein's equations. The Schwarzschild and Reissner-Nordstr\"om geometries are
central examples. Their symmetry makes the geodesic motion of test particles
regular. Enough conserved quantities, integrals of motion, exist to reduce the dynamics to an
integrable problem. This is mathematically convenient, but the more relevant question is what the analysis of this motion can tell us about more realistic problems. Real astrophysical black holes are not isolated ideal bodies. They are
surrounded by accreting matter, electromagnetic fields, stars, or other compact
objects. We are therefore motivated by astrophysical applications to study systems that can provide insight into this situation. Realistic black holes are commonly approximated by the Kerr metric. However, simpler systems can still provide a useful view. In a series of papers and theses \cite{Semerak2016,Witzany2015,PolcarThesis2018,Polcar2019,PolcarSemerak2019, Kraus2025, Sychrovsky2020}, stationary axially symmetric systems consisting of black holes superposed with external disks or rings were studied. These systems exhibit broken integrability. Hence, irregular and chaotic dynamics is present. The dynamics was analyzed using many methods, such as Poincaré sections, the Melnikov method, or basin map analysis.

The astrophysical motivation is therefore not that the thin rings used in this
thesis are literal models of accretion flows. They are controlled idealizations
of external gravitating matter. Their advantage is that they keep the problem
stationary and axially symmetric while still perturbing the black-hole field
strongly enough to test how regular motion is destroyed. In this setting, chaos
is relevant because it limits the predictability of the outcomes. Two nearby initial
conditions can lead to different outcomes, such as a long-lived bounded motion or
termination near the black hole or the ring. Basin maps and fractal dimensions
provide a way to quantify this sensitivity.

This thesis follows the work of David Sychrovsky~\cite{Sychrovsky2020}, who
studied basin maps and basin boundary dimensions for a Schwarzschild black hole
perturbed by external Weyl sources. We want to analyze basin boundary dimensions and basin maps using a different method and extend the analysis to systems not previously studied with basin map analysis.

The thesis is structured as follows:
In chapter \ref{chap:chaos} and \ref{chap:general-relativity} we will introduce the necessary theory that is relevant for this thesis. We will discuss and present our method in chapter \ref{chap:implementation}. The results will be analyzed in chapter \ref{chap:results}.
